Go-to-Market Strategy

A go-to-market (GTM) strategy is the plan for how a product reaches its target customers. Every new product launch, market entry, and significant pivot needs a GTM strategy — otherwise the business builds something, calls it "marketing's job to figure out", and wonders why adoption is slow.

The Marketing Director owns the GTM strategy in collaboration with Sales and Product. Marketing is not a support function at launch time — it is a core architect of how the product reaches the market.

GTM Strategy Framework

A complete GTM strategy answers six questions:

  1. Who is the target customer? (ICP — not a broad statement, a specific definition)
  2. What problem does the product solve? (In the customer's words, not the product team's)
  3. What is the positioning? (Why this product, not a competitor or the status quo)
  4. How will the customer discover and buy the product? (Channels and motion)
  5. What does success look like and when? (Milestones and metrics)
  6. What does each team need to do and by when? (Execution plan)

Market Segmentation and ICP Prioritisation

Before launching, define the priority segment — the beachhead. Every successful market expansion starts narrow and expands.

Segmentation criteria:

  • Pain severity: Which segment has the most acute problem that this product solves?
  • Budget accessibility: Which segment has the budget and willingness to pay?
  • Sales motion fit: Which segment can be reached through the motion we can execute?
  • Competitive white space: Where are we most differentiated vs the status quo?
  • Reference-ability: Which segment, if won, creates the most compelling reference customers?

SA market segmentation considerations:

  • Tier the market by size: enterprise (R5B+ revenue), upper mid-market (R500M–R5B), mid-market (R50M–R500M), SME
  • Enterprise SA often requires formal procurement (tender processes, preferred vendor lists, long timelines)
  • Mid-market is the fastest-growing segment and most accessible to challenger products
  • Government and SOE (State-Owned Enterprises): high value but extremely long cycles; procurement compliance is non-negotiable (PFMA, PPPFA)

Channel Strategy

The channel strategy determines how the product gets from the company to the customer. For B2B:

Direct (Company to Customer)

  • Inside sales (SDR/BDR → AE motion)
  • Field sales (enterprise, high-touch, in-person in SA)
  • Self-serve / product-led growth (PLG): customer signs up and buys without sales involvement

Indirect (via Partners)

  • Resellers and value-added resellers (VARs): partners resell your product, typically adding services
  • Systems integrators (SIs): large consulting firms that embed your product in implementations
  • Technology partners: complementary products that bundle or refer
  • Distributors: for hardware or high-volume software licensing

SA channel specifics:

  • Reseller networks are important for enterprise SA — large IT distributors (Axiz, Tarsus Technology, First Distribution) have established relationships with corporate buyers
  • SI relationships: Deloitte, Accenture, PwC, Dimension Data, T-Systems all have significant SA enterprise footprint
  • Government sales almost always require a local partner with SITA accreditation or Transversal contract
  • B-BBEE status of channel partners affects the buyer's own B-BBEE scorecard — a B-BBEE Level 1 or 2 partner is a commercial advantage

Pricing Positioning

Pricing is a marketing decision as much as a commercial one — the price communicates value, segment, and positioning.

Pricing models for software/services in SA:

ModelWhen to use
Flat subscriptionSimple, predictable; best for SME/mid-market
Per seat/userScales with customer growth; common in B2B SaaS
Usage-basedAligns cost to value; strong PLG fit
Tiered (Good/Better/Best)Serves multiple segments; anchoring effect
Enterprise customComplex deployments; relationship-based

ZAR pricing considerations:

  • Price anchoring in ZAR, not USD, for SA SME and mid-market (USD pricing creates friction for non-finance buyers)
  • Enterprise SA often prices in USD for contract stability given ZAR volatility
  • VAT: all B2B prices should be stated as ex-VAT; add 15% on the invoice

Value-based pricing: Set price based on the value delivered to the customer, not cost-plus. A product that saves a customer R5M per year can be priced at R500K (10× ROI) — which is only expensive relative to cost, not relative to value.

The Launch Plan

A product launch is not a single event — it is a 3-phase process:

Phase 1 — Pre-launch (6–8 weeks before)

  • Seed the market: Brief key journalists and analysts under embargo; share with beta customers for case studies
  • Build the launch assets: Website updates, sales collateral, email templates, social posts, press release, demo video
  • Prepare sales team: Training, battlecards, objection handling, demo certification
  • Build the launch list: Email contacts, social audiences, media targets, partner contacts who will amplify

Phase 2 — Launch week

  • Day 1: Press release + media outreach; social announcement; email to existing customers and list; sales team outreach to warm pipeline
  • Day 2–3: Partner announcements, customer amplification (ask happy customers to share)
  • Week 1: LinkedIn live or webinar covering the product; content publishing (blog, video)

Phase 3 — Post-launch (4–8 weeks)

  • Monitor and respond: news coverage, social mentions, customer feedback
  • Amplify: find the coverage and comments that are getting traction; support them
  • Gather signal: which segments are converting? Which messages are resonating? Adjust
  • Feed product: capture objections and friction from early adopters; route to product team

Market Entry (New Geography or Segment)

Entering a new geography (e.g., a SA company expanding to Kenya, Nigeria, or the UK) requires a separate GTM strategy, not just a language change on the website.

Market entry checklist:

  • [ ] Local regulatory requirements understood (company registration, data residency, VAT/tax)
  • [ ] Pricing model adapted to local market purchasing power and competitive set
  • [ ] Target segment defined (same ICP as SA? Or different segment for market entry?)
  • [ ] Channel partners identified (local distributor, reseller, or SI relationship)
  • [ ] Local PR and media contacts established
  • [ ] Initial reference customers targeted (win 2–3 lighthouse customers before scaling)
  • [ ] Local language and cultural adaptation assessed (not just translation)
  • [ ] Go-live milestone defined: what does "success in year 1" look like?

Pan-African expansion specifics:

  • Each African market is distinct — strategies that work in SA do not translate directly to Nigeria, Kenya, or Egypt
  • Currency risk in markets with volatile currencies (NGN, KES, EGP) — price in USD for enterprise; in local currency for SME
  • Payment infrastructure varies dramatically — M-Pesa in Kenya, local bank transfer in Nigeria, card acceptance patchy across markets
