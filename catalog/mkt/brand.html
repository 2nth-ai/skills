Brand Strategy & Positioning

Brand is the promise you make before the sales conversation starts. A strong brand reduces CAC, commands price premium, and creates a moat that competitors cannot buy. A weak brand means every deal starts from zero — price negotiation, competitor comparison, and distrust. The Marketing Director owns the brand and fights for it in every business decision.

Brand Positioning

Positioning answers three questions a customer unconsciously asks:

  1. What is this? — The category
  2. Who is it for? — The target customer
  3. Why this one? — The point of difference

Positioning statement template:

For [target customer] who [pain or desire], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe].

A good positioning statement is not a tagline — it is an internal strategic document that governs all messaging decisions. It should be hard to write and rarely changed.

Common positioning failures:

  • Positioning against a competitor ("the cheaper alternative to X") — you become the second-best version of what they built
  • Positioning too broadly ("for everyone") — you are distinctive to no one
  • Claiming benefits that every competitor claims ("quality", "reliable", "innovative")

Messaging Architecture

A messaging hierarchy ensures every touchpoint — from a tweet to a proposal — reinforces the same core idea.

Three-level hierarchy:

1. Core Narrative (North Star)

The single most important thing the market should believe about your brand. One sentence. Unforgettable. True. Example: "The world's fastest logistics platform for African commerce."

2. Pillars (3-4 Supporting Claims)

The key proof points that make the core narrative credible. Each pillar gets its own evidence, stories, and proof.

PillarMessageProof
Speed"Ship in 24 hours"Average delivery time data
Coverage"50 cities across Africa"City list, coverage map
Reliability"99.2% on-time"SLA data, customer testimonials

3. Proof Points

Data, case studies, awards, third-party validation. Specific beats general. "Reduced client's time-to-market by 42%" beats "improved efficiency."

Brand Identity Management

The visual identity is the brand's face — colour palette, typography, logo system, photography style, iconography.

What marketing actually owns:

  • Brand guidelines document (the living rulebook)
  • Approval for brand applications (agency briefs, partner co-branding)
  • Brand asset library (logo files, templates, photography)
  • Brand audit: quarterly check that all touchpoints are on-brand

Common violations that erode brand equity:

  • Sales team creating their own slide decks (off-brand templates)
  • Social posts using wrong fonts or colours
  • Website copy written without the messaging framework
  • Agencies "refreshing" visual elements without brand review

Brand vs Demand

One of the most important and most fought-over budget debates in B2B marketing.

Brand MarketingDemand Generation
Builds category ownership over timeCaptures existing demand now
Hard to attribute directly to revenueAttribution to pipeline
Audience: future buyers not yet in marketAudience: active buyers now
Timelines: 6–18 months to measurable impactTimelines: weeks to months

The evidence (Binet & Field's "The Long and the Short of It"): optimal B2B split is approximately 40–60% brand / 60–40% demand depending on market maturity. Cutting brand entirely to hit short-term targets creates a marketing debt that is expensive to repay.

Brand Equity Measurement

Brand is not unmeasurable — it is just measured differently from demand.

Leading indicators:

  • Aided and unaided awareness — survey-based; track quarterly
  • Share of voice (SOV) — your mentions vs competitors in key media and social
  • Brand search volume — Google Trends / Search Console; direct intent signal
  • Net Promoter Score (NPS) — correlates strongly with brand strength
  • Consideration and preference — qualitative research; tracks conversion probability

Tracking frequency: Brand trackers run quarterly at minimum; major campaigns warrant pre/post measurement.

Brand Architecture

When the business grows, brand architecture questions emerge:

  • Monolithic (House of Brand): Everything under one master brand (Virgin, Google). Strongest brand equity centralisation; each sub-product borrows trust from the master brand.
  • Endorsed: Sub-brands with a visible parent endorsement (Marriott / Courtyard by Marriott). Allows product differentiation while preserving parent equity.
  • Pluralistic (Branded House): Independent brands with no visible parent (Unilever, P&G). Best for different audiences who must not know they are the same company.

For most SMBs: start monolithic. Complexity only adds value when the different products genuinely require separate positioning.
