B2B Demand Generation

Demand generation is the practice of creating and accelerating pipeline — both by building awareness in buyers who do not yet know they need your solution (creating demand) and capturing buyers who are already in market (demand capture). In B2B, this is the marketing function most directly connected to revenue.

Demand Creation vs Demand Capture

Most B2B marketing teams over-invest in demand capture (bottom of funnel, high-intent keywords, retargeting active buyers) and under-invest in demand creation (brand building, thought leadership, category creation).

The problem: demand capture is a zero-sum game. You compete for the same pool of buyers your competitors are also targeting. Demand creation expands the pool of buyers — and you are the brand they know when they become ready to buy.

The 95/5 rule (LinkedIn B2B Institute research): Only 5% of your target market is actively in-market at any given time. The 95% who are not buying now will be buying eventually — and they will choose the brand they remember. This is why brand investment is not optional.

Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

Before generating leads, define exactly who you want.

ICP dimensions:

  • Firmographics: Industry, company size (revenue and headcount), geography, growth stage
  • Technographics: Technology stack (signals readiness and budget)
  • Behavioural: Recent funding, hiring patterns, new executive hire, entering a new market
  • Psychographics: Buyer's orientation to risk, innovation, DIY vs managed solution

SA ICP considerations:

  • Company size segmentation: South Africa has a thin layer of large enterprise, a small mid-market, and a vast SME base. Segment carefully — enterprise and SME require completely different sales motions
  • Geography: Gauteng is the commercial hub; Western Cape growing rapidly; other metros have meaningful business populations but longer sales cycles due to fewer local peers
  • Sector concentration: Financial services (banks, insurance), public sector/SOEs, mining, retail, telecom — these dominate large SA enterprise spend

Account-Based Marketing (ABM)

ABM inverts the traditional funnel — instead of casting wide and filtering down, you identify target accounts first and orchestrate campaigns specifically for them.

ABM tiers:

TierAccountsInvestment per accountApproach
1:1 (Strategic)5–20 named accountsVery highFully bespoke: custom content, executive engagement, personalised campaigns
1:few (Cluster)20–100 accountsMediumSemi-personalised: content by vertical, targeted ads, coordinated outreach
1:many (Programmatic)100–1,000 accountsLowPersonalised only by segment: industry-specific landing pages, targeted ads

ABM in SA: The enterprise market is small enough to make 1:1 ABM viable for most categories. A serious pursuit of the top 20 SA banks, or the top 30 SA retailers, or the 15 largest SA mining groups is a defined, finite list — not a needle in a haystack.

ABM toolkit:

  • LinkedIn Matched Audiences (upload account list → target only those companies)
  • Intent data tools (Bombora, 6sense): identify when target accounts are researching your category
  • Personalised landing pages by account or cluster
  • Direct mail (physical): high-response in a digital-saturated world; used for top 1:1 accounts
  • Executive-level event invitations

Lead Scoring

Lead scoring assigns points to leads based on demographic fit (ICP match) and behavioural engagement (interest signals), producing a score that predicts sales readiness.

Two-dimensional scoring:

DimensionWhat it measuresExample signals
Fit scoreICP alignmentJob title, company size, industry, geography
Engagement scoreInterest levelPage visits, content downloads, email opens, webinar attendance

Scoring thresholds:

  • MQL (Marketing Qualified Lead): Fit ≥ 60 AND Engagement ≥ 40. Ready for SDR/BDR outreach.
  • SQL (Sales Qualified Lead): MQL that has been contacted, need confirmed, timeline and budget indicated. Ready for Account Executive.

Lead scoring in practice:

  • Review and calibrate scores quarterly against actual win rates (do high-score leads convert at higher rates?)
  • Sales feedback loop is critical: if SDRs consistently say MQLs are poor quality, the scoring model needs adjustment
  • Negative scoring: certain behaviours (competitor job title, wrong geography, competitor email domain) should reduce score

MQL/SQL Definition and Marketing-Sales Alignment

The most common source of marketing-sales conflict is disagreement about what constitutes a "good" lead.

The MQL/SQL service level agreement (SLA):

  • Marketing commits to: X MQLs per month at a quality standard defined jointly with sales
  • Sales commits to: Follow up on every MQL within Y hours; provide feedback within Z days
  • Shared metric: MQL → SQL conversion rate (if below 20%, either leads are poor quality or sales follow-up is too slow/poor)

Joint MQL definition process:

  1. Pull the last 12 months of closed/won deals
  2. Identify what those buyers had in common at lead stage (job title, company size, behaviour)
  3. Use this data — not assumptions — to set the scoring model

Lead Nurture

Not all leads are ready to buy. Nurture keeps your brand in front of them until they are.

Nurture track design:

  • Track 1 — New inbound lead: 5–8 emails over 4–6 weeks; educational content aligned to their persona; soft CTA for demo/call at email 4 and 8
  • Track 2 — Event/content leads (lower intent): 8–12 emails over 8–12 weeks; longer education phase before sales outreach
  • Track 3 — Re-engagement: Inactive MQLs (no engagement in 90 days); 3-email re-engagement sequence before moving to cold outreach or archiving

SA nurture specifics:

  • WhatsApp Business is beginning to replace email nurture in some SA B2B contexts for mid-market — higher open rates but requires opt-in and careful cadencing
  • Longer nurture windows than US/UK market: SA B2B buyers often have longer decision cycles due to committee buying and fiscal year constraints (government buyers follow a March year-end)

Pipeline Contribution Reporting

The demand gen function should produce a monthly pipeline report showing:

  1. Leads generated by channel and quality tier (MQL, SQL)
  2. Pipeline created by marketing-sourced and influenced opportunities (in ZAR)
  3. Pipeline velocity — average deal size × win rate × volume / average sales cycle length
  4. Cost per MQL / SQL by channel — where is lead quality highest relative to cost?
  5. Funnel stage conversion rates — where are leads dropping out?

This is the language the CFO and CEO understand. Marketing that cannot produce this report is marketing that is vulnerable to budget cuts.
