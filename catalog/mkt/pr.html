Public Relations & Communications

PR is earned media — coverage that money cannot directly buy and that readers trust more than advertising because of it. A well-placed article in Business Day or a keynote slot at AfricaCom does more for brand credibility than any equivalent spend on display advertising. The CMO's job is to build a PR function that consistently generates these outcomes, not one that sends press releases into the void.

The PR Story Pyramid

Not everything is a press release. Media relations require understanding what journalists actually want to publish.

Tier 1 — Newsworthy (high interest)

  • Major funding round or acquisition
  • Significant new client (named, with permission)
  • Original research or survey data with SA relevance
  • Response to a major current event in your category
  • Executive appointment at C-suite level
  • Launch that affects a broad audience

Tier 2 — Interesting (possible placement with right angle)

  • Product launch with a genuine differentiator
  • Case study with quantified results (customer must be named)
  • Expert commentary on a trending news story
  • Award or recognition from a credible body

Tier 3 — Not news (internal milestone)

  • General company updates without external impact
  • Minor product features
  • Standard partnership announcements without a story
  • Rebrands with no strategic narrative

Rule: Write a Tier 3 story and you will not get placed. Write a Tier 1 story and journalists will find you.

Building Media Relationships

South Africa's business media community is small. The same journalists cover a category for years. Relationship-based PR — knowing the right journalist before you have a story — is worth more than any press release.

How to build media relationships:

  • Follow key journalists on LinkedIn and engage genuinely with their work (not just on days you have a story)
  • Offer them a briefing on your category, no story required — become a background source
  • Share data or insights that make their job easier
  • Respond immediately when they reach out for comment on breaking news
  • Introduce them to interesting founders or executives in your network

What not to do:

  • Blast a press release to 200 journalists with no personalisation
  • Follow up a non-response with six chase emails
  • Pitch a story that is obviously only interesting to your company
  • Ask for approval of the article before publication (journalists will decline)

Press Releases

A press release is a last-resort tool, not a first step. Use it when:

  • The news is genuinely newsworthy (Tier 1)
  • You need a paper trail for compliance reasons
  • You are distributing through a wire service (PR Newswire, African Newsroom) for SEO or investor purposes

Press release structure:

  • Headline: What happened, in one sentence, in the active voice. Not "XYZ Company Announces Strategic Partnership" — try "XYZ and ABC Combine to Bring AI-Powered Lending to South African SMEs"
  • Lead paragraph: Who, what, when, where, why — everything a reader needs in the first 50 words
  • Quote (key executive): A genuine quote with a point of view, not a corporate platitude
  • Context: Industry background, company context, deal terms if relevant
  • Customer/partner quote: If applicable
  • Boilerplate: Standard company description paragraph; keeps it consistent
  • Contact: Journalist contact (PR manager or agency, not CEO)

Thought Leadership Placement

Getting executives published in business media is one of the highest-value PR activities. A bylined article in Business Day or Moneyweb:

  • Is trusted more than advertising
  • Lives online permanently (SEO value)
  • Can be shared in sales conversations
  • Positions the executive as a category authority

How to land a placement:

  1. Pitch the editor, not the press release inbox: Find the section editor for the topic and email them directly with a 3-sentence pitch (topic, angle, why your executive is the right person to write this)
  2. Lead with a contrarian point of view: "Why South African CFOs are wrong about AI" will get more interest than "The benefits of AI for finance"
  3. Tie to a news hook: Connect the piece to something already in the news cycle
  4. Do not pitch what has already been published: Read the publication; check if the angle is fresh

SA placement fees: Business Day and Moneyweb do not pay for contributed articles from companies. Mail & Guardian occasionally commissions. BizCommunity accepts free contributions. ITWeb accepts contributed content for their platform.

Crisis Communications

A crisis is any event that could significantly damage the brand's reputation if not managed correctly. The Marketing Director owns the communications response; they do not own the crisis decision (that is the CEO and legal).

Crisis classification:

  • Tier 1 (Immediate): Data breach, serious employee incident, product failure causing harm, regulatory investigation. Act within hours.
  • Tier 2 (Urgent): Significant customer complaint going viral, media investigation, executive misconduct allegation. Act within 24 hours.
  • Tier 3 (Managed): Negative coverage, minor social media backlash, disgruntled former employee. Monitor and respond proportionally.

Crisis communications principles:

  1. Respond, do not react: Gather facts before speaking. A wrong statement is worse than a delayed one — but silence for more than a few hours reads as guilt.
  2. One spokesperson, one message: Contradictory statements from different executives are catastrophic.
  3. Acknowledge first, explain later: "We are aware and are investigating" is always better than defending the indefensible before you know what happened.
  4. Do not escalate it: Responding aggressively to media or customer criticism on social media turns a small fire into an inferno.
  5. Fix the problem, then tell people you fixed it: Reputational recovery requires a demonstrated change, not just an apology.

Crisis response template:

"We are aware of [issue]. We take this seriously and have [immediate action taken]. We are investigating fully and will provide an update within [timeframe]. Our first priority is [affected party/resolution]."
