# Mental Health Services

Mental health is the most under-resourced sector in South African healthcare. The treatment gap — the proportion of people with a mental disorder who receive no treatment — exceeds 75%. A healthcare director operating in any clinical setting will encounter mental health presentations, legal obligations under the Mental Health Care Act, and the consequences of a system stretched beyond capacity.

---

## Mental Health Care Act 17 of 2002 (MHCA)

The MHCA defines three categories of mental health care user and the pathway for each:

### 1. Voluntary Mental Health Care User

A person who has the capacity to consent to mental health treatment and does so willingly.

- May be admitted and discharged at their own request
- Must be informed of their rights before admission
- Consent requirements: written informed consent; must be competent to consent
- No special procedural requirements beyond general consent law
- Can leave against medical advice (AMA) — document refusal clearly; assess capacity

### 2. Assisted Mental Health Care User

A person who does not have the capacity to consent but does not refuse care — or a person who is a minor whose parent/guardian consents on their behalf.

**Criteria**: Lacks capacity to consent AND does not resist; OR is a minor.

**Process**:
1. Two healthcare practitioners complete Form 04 (application for assisted care) and Form 05 (medical report)
2. Head of health establishment admits and notifies the Mental Health Review Board within 7 days
3. Board reviews within 30 days; may confirm, vary, or discharge
4. Maximum 30 days without Review Board review

### 3. Involuntary Mental Health Care User

A person who has a mental illness AND refuses care AND poses a risk to self or others.

**Criteria (s33 — all three must be met)**:
1. Has a mental illness
2. Does not have capacity to consent OR refuses consent
3. Likely to cause serious harm to self or others if not treated

**Process**:

```
Step 1 — 72-hour assessment (s40)
  Any person may apply to a health establishment for assessment
  Two mental health practitioners complete Form 01 (application) and Form 02 (referral)
  Patient assessed at the health establishment for up to 72 hours
                    ↓
Step 2 — Finding
  Not mentally ill → discharge
  Mentally ill but no involuntary criteria → voluntary/assisted pathway
  Meets involuntary criteria → proceed to 28-day observation
                    ↓
Step 3 — 28-day observation (s33)
  Head of establishment completes Form 06 (application for involuntary care)
  Two psychiatrists complete Form 07 (medical report)
  Patient may be detained for up to 28 days for further observation/treatment
                    ↓
Step 4 — Review Board hearing
  Board must convene within 14 days of receipt of application
  Board may: confirm involuntary status, change to voluntary/assisted, discharge
  Patient (and representative) has right to be heard
                    ↓
Step 5 — Continued care
  If Board confirms: patient may be kept for treatment
  Review required every 6 months
  Patient may apply for review at any time
```

### Emergency Admission (s40)

A police officer or health professional may take a person to a health establishment without the usual forms if:
- The person appears to be mentally ill
- Is likely to cause serious harm to self or others
- Requires immediate emergency intervention

The 72-hour assessment clock starts on arrival.

### Mental Health Review Boards

Each health district must have a Mental Health Review Board:
- **Composition**: Magistrate (or retired judge) as chairperson; medical practitioner; mental health care professional (nurse/psychologist/social worker); community representative; legal representative
- **Powers**: Review, confirm, vary, or rescind involuntary and assisted care orders; investigate complaints; inspect facilities
- **Appeal**: Decisions may be appealed to the High Court

### Rights of Mental Health Care Users (s10)

Under the MHCA, mental health care users retain the right to:
- Dignity and respect
- Access to information about their condition, treatment, and rights
- Consent or refuse treatment (if competent)
- Privacy and confidentiality
- Protection from exploitation, abuse, and degradation
- Appropriate treatment in the least restrictive environment
- Legal representation at Review Board proceedings

## Mental Health Burden in South Africa

### Prevalence

- **1 in 6** South Africans has a diagnosable mental disorder in any given year (South African Stress and Health Study — SASH)
- Treatment gap: **>75%** receive no treatment
- **Suicide rate**: ~23.5 per 100,000 — among the highest globally (global average ~9/100,000)
- SADAG (South African Depression and Anxiety Group) estimates 6.6 million people have depression at any given time

### High-Burden Conditions in SA

| Condition | Prevalence | SA-Specific Factors |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Major Depressive Disorder | ~9% lifetime | GBV, poverty, HIV-related |
| Anxiety disorders | ~16% lifetime | Violence exposure, trauma |
| PTSD | Elevated | High interpersonal violence, GBV, crime |
| Bipolar disorder | ~1–2% | High healthcare burden when untreated |
| Schizophrenia spectrum | ~1% | High readmission rate; community support lacking |
| Substance use — alcohol | ~10% AUD | SA among highest global alcohol consumption |
| Substance use — methamphetamine (tik) | Significant in WC | Cape Town highest tik prevalence in Africa |
| Substance use — nyaope/whoonga | Significant in KZN, GP | Opioid + cannabis + ARV — treatment complex |
| ADHD | ~5–8% children | Underdiagnosed and undertreated in SA |

## Service Delivery Levels

### Continuum of Care

```
Community
  → CHW support / WBOT mental health first aid
  → General practitioner (primary care; anxiety, depression, stable schizophrenia)
  → Community Day Centre (intensive outpatient, vocational rehab)
                ↓
District Hospital
  → 72-hour assessment unit
  → Acute psychiatric beds (norm: 23 beds per 100,000 population — rarely achieved)
  → Outpatient psychiatric clinic
                ↓
Regional Hospital
  → Acute psychiatric unit (more complex; ECT capability)
  → Liaison psychiatry (mental health in medical/surgical settings)
                ↓
Specialised Psychiatric Hospital (e.g., Weskoppies, Sterkfontein, Fort England)
  → Long-stay patients (treatment-resistant; forensic; intellectually disabled)
  → Forensic psychiatric units (Section 78 and 79 — Mental Health Care Act: state patients)
```

### Private Sector Mental Health

- Private psychiatric hospitals: Akeso (largest private psychiatric group), Life Mental Health, Netcare Akeso
- Medical aid authorisation for inpatient psychiatric admissions: PMB condition (major depressive disorder, bipolar, schizophrenia)
- PMB psychiatric inpatient: typically 21 days acute authorisation; extended with motivation
- Sub-acute psychiatric care: step-down; fewer acute hospitals; gap in the SA private landscape

## Clinical Frameworks

### Diagnostic Systems

SA uses **ICD-10** (International Classification of Diseases, 10th edition) as the official classification. The DSM-5 is widely used clinically and for research. Note: **courts use ICD-10** when mental health is raised in legal proceedings.

### Common Clinical Presentations and Management Pointers

**Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)**
- First-line: SSRI (fluoxetine, escitalopram) + structured psychotherapy (CBT)
- Treatment-resistant (failed 2 adequate trials): consider ECT, augmentation (lithium, atypical antipsychotic), or referral to psychiatry
- Suicidality assessment: Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale (C-SSRS); safety planning; hospitalise if high risk

**Bipolar Disorder**
- Mood stabiliser: lithium (first-line for bipolar I) or valproate or lamotrigine
- Acute mania: antipsychotic + mood stabiliser ± benzodiazepine
- Lithium monitoring: levels (0.6–1.0 mmol/L therapeutic), TFTs 6-monthly, creatinine 6-monthly
- Teratogenicity: valproate is highly teratogenic (avoid in women of childbearing age without effective contraception)

**Schizophrenia Spectrum**
- First-line antipsychotic: haloperidol (EML), risperidone, or olanzapine
- Depot antipsychotics for adherence: fluphenazine decanoate, zuclopenthixol decanoate, paliperidone palmitate
- Clozapine: for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (failed 2 antipsychotics adequate dose/duration)
  - Mandatory FBC: weekly for 18 weeks, then fortnightly for 1 year, then monthly
  - Agranulocytosis risk: 1–2%; fatal if missed — non-negotiable monitoring
- Psychoeducation: family involvement critical for medication adherence and relapse prevention

**PTSD (Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder)**
- High prevalence in SA — interpersonal violence, GBV, violent crime
- First-line: trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT) or EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitisation and Reprocessing)
- Pharmacotherapy: SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine); prazosin for nightmares
- Avoid benzodiazepines for PTSD (worsens long-term outcomes)

**Substance Use Disorders**
- Alcohol use disorder: AUDIT screening; brief intervention (FRAMES); naltrexone or acamprosate for relapse prevention; disulfiram second-line
- Opioid use disorder (nyaope): buprenorphine-naloxone (Suboxone) — limited SA public sector availability; methadone maintenance in select sites
- Methamphetamine (tik): no approved pharmacotherapy; CBT and contingency management; harm reduction

## Workforce

### The Psychiatrist Shortage

- SA has approximately **400 psychiatrists** for ~60 million people — ratio of 1:150,000 (WHO recommends 1:10,000)
- Distribution: heavily concentrated in Gauteng and Western Cape; many rural districts have no psychiatrist
- Strategy: task-shifting to psychiatric nurses (PCNPs — Primary Care Nurse Practitioners trained in mental health) and clinical psychologists; mhGAP (WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme) training for non-specialist health workers

### mhGAP (Mental Health Gap Action Programme)

WHO's approach to scaling up mental health care in low-resource settings. SA has adapted mhGAP guidelines for training CHWs, nurses, and general practitioners to identify and manage:
- Depression
- Psychosis
- Epilepsy
- Child and adolescent mental health
- Substance use
- Dementia
- Self-harm/suicide

## Workplace Mental Health

### Healthcare Worker Mental Health

Burnout, PTSD, depression, and substance use are elevated among healthcare workers — a global phenomenon intensified in SA by understaffing, violence in the workplace, and resource constraints.

**Management obligations**:
- OHSA (Occupational Health and Safety Act 85 of 1993): employers must provide a safe working environment, including psychological safety
- EAPs (Employee Assistance Programmes): many hospitals contract EAP providers; uptake is often poor due to stigma and confidentiality concerns

### EAP Governance

An effective EAP for a healthcare facility:
- Offers confidential short-term counselling (6–8 sessions)
- Provides 24/7 crisis line
- Covers financial wellness (a major stress driver)
- Has clear referral pathway to medical/psychiatric care when needed
- Reports only aggregate data to management — never individual details

### Fitness for Duty

When a healthcare professional has a mental health condition affecting their practice:
- HPCSA Health Committee (separate from conduct) manages impaired practitioners
- Voluntary health programmes allow treatment without disciplinary action
- Mandatory reporting obligation if a colleague poses patient safety risk — balance against duty of confidentiality
